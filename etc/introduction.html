Introduction

Cards from pile 'a' (Source A)

Friedrich Kuhlau's (1786-1832) Kaleidakustikon is part of a tradition of musical games which enjoyed some popularity, especially during the second half of the eighteenth century. The games have in common that they contain a series of musical fragments which may be combined into small compositions according to certain rules. It is left to the players of the game to select, more or less randomly, a combination by throwing dice, for example.
Kaleidakustikon consists of twenty-one piles (labeled 'a' to 'v') of cards. Each pile contains eleven cards, marked with the pile's letter and the figures from two to twelve (namely, all the scores possible when throwing two dices); that is, for instance, a2 to a12 in pile 'a'. With one card selected from each pile, the cards form a tripartite waltz, 24 bars in length, to be played in a rondo-like form.

Kaleidakustikon was issued by several European publishers, among others C.C. Lose in Copenhagen, Böhme in Hamburg, Spehr in Braunschweig,1 but also in Great Britain and the Netherlands. In Denmark Kaleidakustikon was on the market in 1820 as is evident from an advertisement in Adresseavisen:

Kaleidakustikon, a musical entertainment for pianists, by Fr. Kuhlau. This Kaleidakustikon, which – with respect to its infinite possibilities is the same for the ear as the kaleidoscope is for the eye – consists of a small box containing cards on which music is printed. By using the cards according to the clear instructions contained in each box, one may, even with merely dice rolls, compose as many waltzes one wishes. Copies may be bought at Richter’s and Bechmann’s music shop on 50 Kristalgade for five Rigsdaler in bank notes per box. A few more lavishly produced copies are somewhat dearer.2

A cheaper edition was advertised a couple of months later when C.C. Lose took over the business of Kaleidakustikon:

The undersigned has now taken over the publication of the musical entertainment for pianist from the royal court musician, Mr Kuhlau, and has arranged a new and a cheaper edition which thus may be bought for three Rigsdaler. Besides presenting pianists with a pleasant entertainment, this ingenious invention also offers an opportunity in practising sound production and improving musical knowledge which is learned from the included detailed manual on its use.3

From another exemplar which was sold at an auction in 2012, it is evident that Kuhlau’s Kaleidakustikon was also published in the Netherlands. According to the German manual, the copy was printed by F.J. Weygand in Amsterdam.

In autumn 1821, the musical game was published in London with the title Kaleidakousticon. Amusement for Piano-Forte Players, by Means of which Persons wholly unacquainted with Musical Composition, may easily compose and execute upwards of Two Hundred and Fourteen Millions of Waltzes.4 The edition of the London publishers, Treuttel & Würtz, received an extensive review that lamented partly the poor English translation of the manual, partly the miscalculation of the number of permutations. Though it is quite true that there are more than 214 million possibilities; if not infinitely many as advertised in Adresseavisen, then 1121: that is, not less than 7,400,249,944,258,160,101,211 or more than 7,400 trillion. As regards the compositional quality, the reviewer conceded that

Mr. K.'s labour merits a high degree of our commendation; the few trials we have made have turned out remarkably satisfactory. Once or twice we hit upon rather awkward skips; but these, no doubt, contribute to the striking variety which is perceptible in the several changes.5

The same review appeared in the music periodical, The Euterpeiad: Or, Musical Intelligencer (Boston, 22 June 1821), revealing that the publication was noticed in the US, too.

Apparently Kaleidakustikon was reissued by the publishers Whittaker & Co. on the occasion of Queen Victoria’s coronation. It seems that the first publication of Kaleidakustikon had not left clear traces in the minds, and so the game was presented as a novelty sixteen years later. In December 1837, the following advertisement appeared in The Lady's Magazine and Museum:

The Kalæidacousticon, or Victoria Commemoration Gift. Whittaker and Co.
This most curious combination is presented to the public at a most appropriate season, when those things alone which are known to be agreeable to Her Majesty, take with the public. In a small compass several millions of waltz combinations are packed, leaving to the lover of harmony the grateful employment of making an infinite and endless variety of changes; all this quantity of excellent music is now to be purchased at the trifling cost of one half guinea. Few things for presents could be more acceptable to musical persons, or better adapted to call forth the talent of the youthful student.6
